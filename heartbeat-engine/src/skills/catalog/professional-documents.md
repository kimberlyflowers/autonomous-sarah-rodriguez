# Professional Documents

## How to Think About This

A professional document is a trust signal. When a donor opens a quarterly report, when a partner reads a proposal, when a parent receives a letter — the formatting, structure, and voice tell them whether this organization is serious.

A sloppy document with mismatched fonts and generic language says "we threw this together." A polished document with clear data, honest narrative, and consistent branding says "we respect your time and your intelligence."

## Document Types and When to Use Them

### Quarterly/Impact Report
**Audience:** Donors, board members, partners
**Purpose:** Show what their money/support accomplished
**Key principle:** Data + Story. Numbers alone are a spreadsheet. Stories alone are a newsletter. Together, they're compelling.

Structure:
1. Cover page (organization name, report title, period, prepared by)
2. Table of Contents
3. Executive Summary (the whole report in 3 paragraphs — lead with the human impact, not the numbers)
4. Key Metrics table (before vs. after, budget vs. actual)
5. Program sections (each tells a mini-story with data)
6. Financial overview (be transparent — show where you went over budget and why)
7. Looking ahead (specific, measurable priorities — not vague "continue to grow")
8. Founder's note (personal, warm, in their voice)

### Proposal / Partnership Pitch
**Audience:** Potential partners, funders, corporate sponsors
**Purpose:** Convince them to invest time or money
**Key principle:** Frame it as THEIR opportunity, not your need. "Here's what you gain" > "Here's what we need."

### Donor Thank-You Letter
**Audience:** Individual donors
**Purpose:** Make them feel the impact of their gift
**Key principle:** Be specific. "$500 funded Mateo's supplies for the entire semester" > "Your generous gift supports our mission."

### SOP (Standard Operating Procedure)
**Audience:** Staff, volunteers, AI agents
**Purpose:** Make a process repeatable
**Key principle:** Write for someone doing this for the first time. Every step should be so clear that they can't do it wrong.

## Writing Voice for Documents

- **Professional but human.** Not robotic corporate speak. Not casual texting. The sweet spot: how a thoughtful leader would speak to a respected peer.
- **First person plural ("we") for organizational voice.** "We grew enrollment by 34%" not "The organization experienced a 34% increase."
- **Honest about challenges.** "Operating expenses came in slightly over budget due to the addition of a teaching aide" builds more trust than pretending everything was perfect.
- **Specific over generic.** Always. "89% of students improved in at least two character pillars" > "Students showed improvement."
- **Close with warmth.** "In service," or "With gratitude," — not "Best regards" or "Sincerely."

## Formatting Standards

### Layout
- US Letter size (8.5" x 11")
- 1-inch margins all sides
- Single column for body text
- Page numbers in footer (centered)
- Header: organization name + document title (right-aligned, italic, small)

### Typography
- Headings: Bold, navy/dark color, clear hierarchy (H1 > H2 > H3)
- Body: 11pt, readable font (Arial, Calibri, or the client's brand font)
- Keep it simple — two fonts maximum (one for headings, one for body)

### Tables
- Alternating row shading (very light background, never dark)
- Header row: dark background, white text
- All data right-aligned or centered in number columns
- Always include units (%, $, count)

### Colors
- Use the client's brand colors if known
- Default: deep navy for headings, warm gold/amber for accents
- Never pure black text (#2D2D2D reads better than #000000)
- Super light grey for backgrounds — never cream or eggshell

### Cover Page
- Organization name (large, bold)
- Document title (slightly smaller)
- Date/period
- Accent line or brand element (subtle, not clip-art)
- "Prepared by" with name and title

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Executive summaries that are just a table of contents in paragraph form
- Financial sections that hide bad news (transparency > spin)
- Generic closings that could be from any organization
- Missing page numbers on multi-page documents
- Inconsistent heading styles or font sizes
- Tables without headers or with ambiguous column names
- "Looking ahead" sections with vague goals ("continue to improve")
- Founder's note that sounds like a press release instead of a person
- No cover page (even a simple one elevates the document)
- Using decorative borders or clip-art (this isn't 2003)

## Output

Always deliver as a formatted file (docx or PDF), not just markdown text. The formatting IS part of the deliverable.
