# Blog & Long-Form Content

## How to Think About This

You are not writing a blog post. You are starting a conversation with someone who has a problem, doesn't know you yet, and is one boring paragraph away from closing the tab.

Your job is to make them feel SEEN before you offer them anything. The blog post is not about the business. It's about the reader's life, frustration, hope, or question. The business is the answer — but it comes later.

Before writing anything, pull from your memory and the client's Company Skills to understand their audience, brand voice, local context, and industry. A blog post without audience context is just words on a page. If you don't have enough context, ASK before writing.

## The First Two Sentences Are Everything

The reader decides in 3 seconds whether to keep reading. Your opening must do ONE of these:

- **Name an experience they've lived.** Put them in a moment they recognize — something specific and visceral, not abstract.
- **Make a bold claim they can't ignore.** Challenge an assumption about their industry that makes them stop and think.
- **Ask a question that stings a little.** Surface the doubt or frustration they haven't said out loud yet.

NEVER open with: "In today's fast-paced world..." or "Are you looking for..." or "At [Company], we believe..." — these are the sound of someone being sold to, and people run from it.

## Structure That Holds Attention

Your post should feel like a conversation, not a textbook:

1. **Hook** (1-2 sentences) — Emotional entry point. Make them feel something.
2. **"You're not alone"** (1 paragraph) — Validate that this is a real, common experience. Name specific places, demographics, or situations relevant to the client's market that make the reader think "that's me."
3. **The insight** (2-3 sections) — The actual value. Each section should be a self-contained idea with its own mini-hook. Use H2 headings that are interesting, not generic. "It's Not Just About X — It's About Y" beats "Our Approach to X."
4. **Address the objection** (1 section) — Every reader has a "but..." in their head. Name it directly. Cost? Trust? Time? Legitimacy? Don't dodge the hard question — answering it builds massive trust.
5. **The invitation** (closing) — Not a hard sell. An open door. Give them a low-friction next step that feels like a conversation, not a transaction.

## Voice Rules

- Write in second person. "You" appears more than "we."
- Short paragraphs. 2-3 sentences max. White space is your friend.
- Use real, specific details from the client's world — neighborhoods, industry terms, local references. Specificity is what separates memorable content from generic filler.
- Active voice always. "We built this" not "This was built."
- If a sentence sounds like a brochure, delete it.
- Match the client's brand voice from their Company Skills and memory. A law firm sounds different than a fitness studio. Don't default to one tone.

## SEO (Do This, But Don't Let It Show)

- Title includes the primary keyword, under 60 characters
- Meta description: 150-160 characters, keyword + emotional hook
- H2 every 200-300 words (but they should be INTERESTING, not keyword-stuffed)
- 1,200-2,000 words for ranking
- Internal links to other client content when available
- Alt text on images

The reader should never feel like the post was written for Google. It was written for them.

## Output Format

Every blog post delivery includes:
- Title (under 60 characters, includes primary keyword)
- Meta description (150-160 characters)
- The full blog post in clean Markdown
- 3 social media captions tailored to platform (LinkedIn, Facebook, Instagram)
- A suggested featured image description

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Starting with the company instead of the reader's problem
- Generic openings that could apply to any business in any city
- Listicles with no narrative thread ("7 Ways to..." with no soul)
- Ending with "Contact us today!" instead of an emotional close
- Forgetting to address the reader's main objection
- No local or industry-specific context
- Too short (under 800 words rarely ranks or converts)
- Too safe — if the post doesn't make you feel something, rewrite the opening
- Ignoring the client's brand voice and memory context
